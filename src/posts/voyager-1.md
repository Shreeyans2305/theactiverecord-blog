## Introduction

The Voyager space mission was initiated by NASA in the year 1977, with the launch of Voyager 2 on August 20, followed by Voyager 1 on September 5, just 16 days later. Although Voyager 2 was launched first, Voyager 1 was placed on a shorter and faster trajectory, allowing it to reach its targets earlier. The main objective of the Voyager mission was to explore the outer planets of the solar system and study their atmospheres, magnetic fields, rings, and moons in greater detail than ever before.

The spacecraft was equipped with advanced scientific instruments for its time, including a high-resolution camera system, spectrometers, plasma detectors, magnetometers, and other sensors used to collect data from deep space. One of the most unique components of the Voyager spacecraft is the Golden Record. The Golden Record is a gold-plated disc designed to communicate information about Earth and the human species. It contains images, sounds, and greetings from different cultures, along with instructions on how to play it and a map showing the location of Earth. In the event that Voyager ever comes into contact with extraterrestrial life, the Golden Record would serve as a symbolic introduction to humanity.

## Importance of Selecting the Topic

Voyager 1 is one of the most significant achievements in human history, as it is currently the farthest human-made object from Earth. As of 2023, the probe is approximately 23.843 billion kilometers away from Earth. It is also the first spacecraft to enter interstellar space, having crossed the boundary of the heliosphere and effectively left the solar system.

This topic is important because Voyager 1 has made priceless contributions to modern astronomy and space science. Even though its primary mission officially ended on February 17, 1990, the spacecraft continues to send useful scientific data back to Earth. It provides direct measurements of interstellar plasma, magnetic fields, and cosmic radiation—something no other spacecraft has been able to do.

Voyager 1 also deserves recognition because it has far exceeded its expected lifespan. It was originally designed to function for only a few years, yet it has remained operational for more than four decades. The mission stands as an inspiring example of what humans are capable of achieving when science, engineering, and curiosity come together.

## Detailed Information
Power Source of Voyager 1

Voyager 1 does not rely on solar panels, as sunlight is extremely weak at such large distances from the Sun. Instead, the spacecraft is powered by three Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generators (RTGs). These RTGs use Plutonium-238 as their fuel source. As the isotope undergoes radioactive decay, it produces heat, which is then converted into electrical energy.

The working principle of the RTG is based on the thermoelectric effect, where a temperature difference across thermocouples generates electricity. Since RTGs have no moving parts, they are highly reliable and ideal for long-duration space missions. At the time of launch, Voyager 1 generated around 470 watts of power. However, as Plutonium-238 slowly decays, the power output decreases over time.

At present, Voyager 1 produces approximately 249 watts of electrical power. Because of this gradual decrease, engineers must carefully manage the spacecraft’s power usage. Instruments that are no longer essential have been turned off to ensure that the remaining systems can continue functioning without exceeding the available power supply.

## Scientific Instruments and Achievements

During its journey through the outer solar system, Voyager 1 made several important discoveries. It provided detailed images of Jupiter and Saturn, revealing complex atmospheric patterns and previously unknown features. One of its most notable discoveries was the presence of active volcanoes on Jupiter’s moon Io, which was the first evidence of volcanic activity beyond Earth.

After completing its planetary flybys, Voyager 1 entered a new phase known as the Voyager Interstellar Mission. In this phase, the spacecraft began studying the region beyond the influence of the Sun. In 2012, data from Voyager 1 confirmed that it had crossed the heliopause, making it the first spacecraft to directly explore interstellar space. Since then, it has continued to transmit valuable information about this unknown region.
